Section 03 · Weakest pillar

Revenue Architecture: 48/100.

The pillar that most limits Pylon's separation from competitors is not brand, not content, not operations. It's the money model. Pylon is running a 19-year-old event business without a subscription layer beneath it, and without monetized use of the deepest asset in the organization.

What's there

Tournament fees and regional league fees drive the P&L. Multiple products (7v7, camps, flag, showcases, NIL) diversify the event mix. Sponsor mentions appear across regional pages. The transactional infrastructure works.

What's missing

Three things. First, no subscription flywheel — every dollar Pylon collects comes from a one-time event registration or a one-time sponsor deal; no recurring revenue rides underneath the event calendar. Second, no published sponsor package structure — deals appear to be negotiated one at a time, which caps deal velocity and inflates negotiation cost per dollar. Third, and biggest: 4,500+ alumni contacts are sitting unmonetized. An NFL alum whose career Pylon helped launch has no formal way to buy back in, refer athletes, or activate against Pylon's youth pipeline in a revenue-share structure.

Why this pillar loses to competitors

Red Zone Elite runs on tournament fees too, but its national media coverage and ranking layer sell sponsor packages at scale. PremierEventsUSA layered a player-card partnership on top of fees, creating a per-athlete recurring line. Midwest BOOM landed an apparel sponsor with an annual value that dwarfs individual event revenue. Pylon has assets bigger than any of these organizations own — and is still monetizing at the event-fee layer.

What fixes it

The alumni graph. Detailed in Section 04.
